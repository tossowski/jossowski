Description of Course

This course provides an introduction to models of computation and the languages they give rise to. After taking this course you will be able to

  • build and analyze finite-state machines, understand and construct non-deterministic automata, construct and analyze regular expressions/languages,
  • minimize finite automata, determine that certain languages are not regular (via application of the Pumping Lemma),
  • construct and analyze pushdown automata, context-free languages, determine that certain languages are not context-free (via the CFL Pumping Lemma),
  • construct Turing Machines, analyze recognizable and decidable languages,
  • prove that certain languages are not decidable or even recognizable,
  • state and understand the intuition behind Church-Turing thesis,
  • measure a Turing Machine's complexity, perform polynomial reductions,
  • identify intractable problems, understand the P vs. NP question.

Attendance

  • Attending lectures in person is an important part of the learning process. Reading the textbook on your own or trying to learn the material primarily from other sources is in practice considerably more difficult than disciplined attendance of the lectures and completion of the course’s homework. In addition, seeing and interacting with your fellow students in person could be very valuable on its own as participating in a group effort is generally stimulating and it may lead to some beneficial serendipitous encounters or ideas. Attendance via Zoom (if available) does not have these benefits and will not count towards your attendance score.

  • For that reason, I will take attendance within the first 15 minutes of every lecture. If you arrive after attendance has been taken, then your participating afterwards will not count towards your attendance score (even though attending could still be very beneficial). The reason for this rule is that in our course we often follow a very defined train of thought where the next idea critically depends on the previous one. As a result, missing the starting part of this thought process will make it very difficult, and most likely impossible, for a late student to catch up. To learn the material, the student will have to re-read or re-watch the material, the remainder of the class being lost (and time is a precious resource). Another reason for this rule is to reduce the number of cases that occurred in the past where some students arrived at the end of a class and requested to be counted as present. Agreeing to that would be unfair to the students who made the effort to attend the lecture from the beginning.
    The goal of this policy is not to be overly meticulous about rules, but to encourage discipline which is very helpful in this challenging course. If you look closely, the policy is generous. Attendance counts for only 4% of the overall grade, and theoretically one can get a good grade even if they miss a substantial number of classes (however, in practice, missing classes is usually a symptom of other, bigger problems and those who miss a lot of classes invariably wind up with poor grades).

  • In addition, our syllabus allows a student to miss 3-lectures for any personal reason without any repercussions on the attendance score (please see the section on personal accommodation for important details). This is to acknowledge that we’re all human and many things can happen during the semester that can reasonably make it difficult to make it to the class on time.
